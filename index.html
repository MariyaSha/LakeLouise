Lake Louise

Alberta, Canada

Join us for an unforgettable journey trough the breathtaking landscapes of Lake Louise.

Wildlife

Lake Louise is home to a wide collection of species. It is important to know the safety rules when encountering wildlife, especially in their inhabitant.
Here are some general guidelines when encountering different species. They are crucial to preserve the animals and to minimize human interaction with the surrounding wild forests.

Bear safety

The Canadian Rocky and Columbia Mountains are home to both grizzly and black bears. You can run into a bear anywhere here, be it on a busy trail close to town or in the remote backcountry. Bears generally prefer to avoid people. However, encounters between bears and people do occur. Knowing how to avoid an encounter with a bear is the best way to enjoy the parks, safely.

If you see a bear

• Stop and remain calm.
• Get ready to use your bear spray.
• Do not run away.

For a complete guide to bears, please visit the Parks Canada Bear Page

Wolf safety

Normally wolves are secretive and will run away when they encounter people, but they can become habituated and may approach camping areas, and park visitors.

If you see a wolf

• Begin scare tactic immediately, well before the wolf is within 100 metres. Do not allow the wolf to approach any closer than 100 metres.
• Raise your arms and wave them in the air to make yourself appear larger.
• Use noise makers (i.e. air horns), throw sticks, rocks and sand at the wolf, to scare wolves away.
• If the wolf displays aggressive behaviour, back away slowly, do not turn your back on the wolf.

For a complete guide to wolves, please visit the BC Parks Wolf Page

Cougar safety

While cougars are a natural predator in the park, they can present a potential risk to people and pets. We live in cougar country and as such must take special precautions, especially residents living on the edge of town or near wooded areas.

If you see a cougar

• Do not approach the animal.
• Face the cougar, and retreat slowly – do not run or play dead.
• Try to appear bigger by holding your arms or an object above your head.
• Immediately pick up small children.
• Be aggressive. Shout, wave a stick or throw rocks to deter an attack.

For a complete guide to cougars, please visit the Parks Canada Cougar Page

Elk safety

Elk can be aggressive and attack without warning. During the fall mating season (Aug - Sept) male are particularly belligerent. During the spring calving season (may - June) female elk aggressively defend their young. DO NOT approach elk in any season as they are DANGEROUS.

If you see an elk

• Always keep a safe distance away from wildlife. We recommend keeping 30 metres, or 3 bus lengths, away from elk for safety.
• If an elk becomes alert or nervous, grinds its teeth or sends its ears back, you're too close - back off.

For a complete guide to elk, please visit the Parks Canada Elk Page

Moose safety

Moose are the largest members of the deer family as well as the tallest land mammals in North America. Attacks on humans by moose are rare, but can occur, especially when a female is protecting her offspring. Vehicle collisions are also a major safety concern associated with moose.

If you see a moose

• Never approach a moose. Give the animals a wide berth and ensure they always have an escape route.
• Be aware of moose body language. A threatened moose may lower its head and flatten its ears before charging. If you see these behaviours, calmly find an escape route.

For a complete guide to moose, please visit the Wildlife Safety BC Moose Page

Bison safety

Bison are wild animals. Even though they look docile they are dangerous, unpredictable, and may charge without warning. Approaching bison is unlawful and puts yourself and others at risk.

If you see a bison

• Keep at least 100 metres (330 feet) away from Bison at all times, even when you are in your vehicle.
• Avoid approaching bison where their escape routes are limited; they may charge more readily.
• If you encounter bison while hiking, don’t try to approach or scare them away. Make them aware of your presence, if they don’t move off- walk well around them, always keeping an eye on them. If necessary, return to the trailhead.

For a complete guide to bison, please visit the Parks Canada Bison Page

Ram safety

Rams are known for charging and headbutting each other. They will also try to attack humans in some circumstances. You can walk toward a ram that begins to back up, stand your ground and dodge an attack, or wrestle it to the ground. Some ways to avoid an attack include keeping your eyes on the ram, avoiding petting a ram, and keeping your head up. Rams can be dangerous so if you don’t know how to handle yourself, it is best to stay away from them.

For a complete guide to ram, please visit the Wiki-How Ram Safety Page

Deer safety

Deer are wild animals, you should never approach deer especially those with young as they may attack. Laying their ears back and lowering their head can be signs of an impending attack. If you are attacked by a deer try to stay upright, cover your head with your arms and move to shelter.

For a complete guide to deer, please visit the Wildlife Safety BC Deer Page

Goose safety

Geese are territorial birds and are known to chase or attack humans who disturb their territory. While geese may chase people, an actual physical attack is fairly rare. You can stop a goose's aggression by respectfully leaving its territory. Back away slowly, while remaining calm. Do not do anything that may escalate the situation, like yelling. In the event you are injured, seek medical attention to assess your injuries.

For a complete guide to geese, please visit the Wiki-How Goose Safety Page
